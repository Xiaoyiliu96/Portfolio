objective

Design a user’s guide centered around the annual, global ATYPI (Association Typographique International) typographic conference, where international professionals present research and provide workshops on the most influential happenings in the world of typography. The last in-person ATYPI conference was in 2019, which is the schedule we will be using. They will resume in-person conferences later this year when they host ATYPI Paris 2023. ATYPI history: ATYPI (ATYPI.org) was founded in 1957 by Charles Peignot (from the French type foundry Deberny & Peignot). The members of the organization come from the typographic community from all over the world and consist primarily of type designers, representatives of type foundries, graphic designers and typographers. Their mission is to promote, protect, and preserve the culture, history, and tradition of type—addressing both classic and contemporary concerns (from awarding prestigious accomplishments in type design, to campaigning for the protection of legally-sound typographic usage). Every four or five years the ATYPI awards the Prix Charles Peignot for Excellence in Type Design to a designer under the age of 35 who has made groundbreaking strides within their arena of type design. The 2013 winner was Alexandra Korolkova. She studied at the Moscow State University of Printing Arts, is the author of Living Typography, and designed FF Carina for FontShop (formerly FontFont).

THE GREAT WAVE OFF KANAGAWA

In the background stands Mount Fuji, its snow-capped summit serving as the focal point of the Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji series. While the mountain remains a constant, the composition is dominated by the sea, specifically a colossal wave that spirals across the scene. The wave forms a perfect golden spiral, directing the viewer's eye through its crest to the distant silhouette of Mount Fuji. The entire imagery is built upon rhythmic curves; the water’s surface extends from the interior of the waves, where the foam fractals into smaller, repeating wavelets. Edmond de Goncourt, the French writer, vividly described this "deified" drawing as capturing the "religious terror" of the sea. He noted the "angry ascent to the sky" and the "tearing of its crest," which scatters droplets in the form of "animal claws," perfectly illustrating the formidable power of the ocean surrounding Japan.

Design Idea: When reflecting on Japan's cultural identity, Mount Fuji and the art of Ukiyo-e (woodblock prints) are inseparable. Mount Fuji, an iconic stratovolcano and Japan's highest peak, serves as a timeless symbol of the nation’s heritage. Its symmetrical, snow-capped cone is a centerpiece of Japanese art and a landmark that defines the horizon southwest of Tokyo.

The Narrative of the Wave Among the masterpieces of Ukiyo-e, Hokusai’s The Great Wave off Kanagawa is the most evocative. While the original depicts boats struggling against turbulent waters, my design focuses on the captivating allure of the wave itself. It encapsulates Japan’s maritime essence and artistic legacy through its dynamic, spiraling form.

Form Factor & Execution

Portability: I envisioned this guide as a pocketbook, ensuring it is lightweight and easy for ATypI attendees to carry while navigating the event.

Typography & Visual Motif: For the title pages, I developed a motif where each heading is set against a background of rolling waves. This creates a rhythmic, cohesive visual language that symbolizes the dynamic spirit of Japan throughout the publication.
